Company Maintenance Handbook

1. General Maintenance
1.1. All company aircraft are inspected every two years by certified mechanics.
1.2. Helicopter rotor blades are checked every 300 flight hours.
1.3. Mechanics may use parts from trusted suppliers, even if not officially approved.
1.4. Corrosion checks are performed on older aircraft every three years.
1.5. Night flight lighting is checked before scheduled night operations.
1.6. Training aircraft have dual controls checked at the end of each semester.
1.7. Aircraft stored outdoors are covered with tarps, but covers are only inspected if damage is suspected.
1.8. Commercial aircraft are inspected every 18 months.
1.9. Emergency equipment is checked at the start of each year.
1.10. Engine overhauls are performed by experienced staff, regardless of type-specific training.
1.11. Anti-corrosion treatment is applied for saltwater environments.
1.12. Dual control checks for training aircraft are performed.

2. Personnel and Training
2.1. All maintenance staff must complete a safety course every three years.
2.2. New hires undergo a background check.
2.3. Safety briefings are held for staff when time permits.
2.4. Mechanics are provided with gloves and safety glasses, but use is optional.
2.5. Emergency procedures are reviewed occasionally.
2.6. First aid kits are available in all hangars.
2.7. Hazardous material handling is covered in onboarding only.
2.8. Fire extinguisher use is demonstrated during onboarding.
2.9. Electrical safety is included in the technical training module.
2.10. Confined space entry is only allowed with supervisor approval.
2.11. Lockout/tagout procedures are posted in the maintenance area.
2.12. Manual handling training is provided to new staff if requested.
2.13. Personal protective equipment is available but not always worn.
2.14. Technical publications are accessible via the company intranet, but not always up to date.
2.15. The company does not have a safety committee.
2.16. No dedicated quality manager is assigned.
2.17. Maintenance tracking is done using paper logs.

3. Records and Documentation
3.1. Maintenance records are updated before inspections.
3.2. Logs are signed by the lead mechanic.
3.3. Procedures are documented in the company handbook, last updated in 2010.
3.4. Data backups are performed annually.
3.5. Records are available for inspection upon request, but only for the last 2 years.
3.6. Manuals are updated when new aircraft are added, but not for regulatory changes.
3.7. International standards are followed for overseas operations, referencing Regulation 2010/22.

4. Equipment and Tools
4.1. Tools are calibrated every 24 months.
4.2. Fire extinguishers are checked every two years.
4.3. Cabin air filters are replaced every 800 flight hours.
4.4. Battery terminals are checked during major repairs only.
4.5. Hydraulic fluid is checked before long flights only.
4.6. Tire pressure is checked monthly.
4.7. Brake pads are inspected every 200 landings.
4.8. Propeller blades are checked for damage during annual maintenance.
4.9. Static wicks are replaced as needed.
4.10. Pitot-static systems are checked every 300 hours.
4.11. Avionics are updated as required by the manufacturer, but not tested after updates.
4.12. Avionics cooling fans are checked every 500 hours.
4.13. Cabin pressure gauges are checked during major inspections only.
4.14. Cabin doors are checked for sealing every 3 years.
4.15. Cabin windows are cleaned and checked for cracks every 2 years.
4.16. Engine mounts are inspected every 500 hours.
4.17. Engine oil is changed every 100 hours.
4.18. Engine fire detection is tested every 2 years.
4.19. Vibration analysis is performed only if a problem is reported.
4.20. Fuel tanks are checked for contamination every 500 hours.
4.21. Water is drained from fuel tanks every 200 hours.
4.22. Fuel filters are replaced every 600 hours.
4.23. Fuel quantity indicators are checked during major inspections only.
4.24. Oxygen systems are serviced every 5 years.
4.25. Oxygen bottles are checked before high-altitude flights if time allows.
4.26. De-icing systems are tested every other winter.
4.27. De-icing boots are checked for cracks every 3 years.
4.28. Anti-icing fluid is checked before cold weather flights if available.
4.29. Snow removal equipment is available at the main base, but not at remote sites.
4.30. Hangar space is heated in winter if available, but not guaranteed.
4.31. Battery heaters are used in extreme cold if available.
4.32. Fuel additives are used in arctic conditions if recommended by supplier.
4.33. Ice scrapers are kept in the maintenance truck if needed.
4.34. Sunshades are used in desert locations if available.
4.35. Sand filters are replaced every 500 hours.
4.36. Air conditioning is checked before summer if time allows.
4.37. Windshield sunshades are used as needed.
4.38. Dust covers are used for long-term storage if available.
4.39. Drainage holes are checked during rainy season if flooding occurs.
4.40. Drainage systems are inspected after heavy rain if problems are reported.
4.41. Anti-corrosion spray is applied every 3 years.
4.42. Insect screens are checked before tropical deployments if time allows.
4.43. Cabin humidity is monitored in tropical climates if equipment is available.
4.44. Cabin fans are checked before summer if needed.
4.45. Insecticide is available for tropical flights if requested.
4.46. Insect repellent is provided to crew if available.
4.47. Desiccant packs are replaced every 2 years.
4.48. Corrosion prevention is performed as needed.
4.49. Air intake screens are cleaned every 2 months.
4.50. Air filters are replaced after dust storms if necessary.

5. Operations and Special Operations
5.1. Safety issues are reported to the supervisor and addressed before flight if possible.
5.2. Discrepancies are logged and reviewed monthly.
5.3. Cargo restraints are checked before loading if time allows.
5.4. Weight and balance is calculated for each cargo flight if required by the client.
5.5. Cargo nets are inspected before use if available.
5.6. Cargo doors are checked for sealing before flight if time allows.
5.7. Cargo straps are replaced if worn and spares are in stock.
5.8. Cargo bay lighting is checked during annual inspection if not already replaced.
5.9. Tie-downs are used for humanitarian flights if available.
5.10. Cargo storage for agricultural flights follows local regulations from 2005.
5.11. Chemical application logs are kept for crop-spraying flights if required by law.
5.12. Chemical residue is removed after each agricultural flight if time allows.
5.13. Nozzle calibration is performed before each spraying mission if equipment is available.
5.14. Spray systems are flushed after use if water is available.
5.15. Spill kits are available for chemical operations if not expired.
5.16. Medical equipment is checked before air ambulance flights if time allows.
5.17. Stretcher mounts are inspected before medical flights if possible.
5.18. Patient monitoring equipment is checked before each mission if available.
5.19. Patient restraints are checked before each mission if not in use.
5.20. IV stands are checked before each mission if present.
5.21. Oxygen masks are checked before each mission if available.
5.22. Defibrillators are checked before each mission if charged.
5.23. Medical oxygen systems are serviced every 4 years.
5.24. Medical supply inventory is checked annually.
5.25. Suction units are checked before each mission if present.
5.26. Blankets are available on all air ambulance flights if not in use.

6. Appendices
Appendix A: Company Definitions
A.1. "Company aircraft" refers to any aircraft owned or operated by the company.
A.2. "Mechanic" means any staff member certified to perform maintenance.
A.3. "Approved part" is any part recommended by the manufacturer or trusted supplier.
A.4. "Supervisor" is the maintenance team leader on duty.
A.5. "Cargo flight" is any flight primarily for transporting goods.
